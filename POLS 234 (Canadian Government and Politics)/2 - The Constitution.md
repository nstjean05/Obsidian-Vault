## 2.1 Constitutions and Their Functions
- **Constitution** - A set of rules that authoritatively give structure and principles to a regime
- These rules are constituted through 4 major functions.
1. **Establish who will exercise political authority**
	- Legislative power - Laws and public policy
	- Executive power - Execute and administer law/policy
	- Judicial power - Settle questions about specific violations of law
2. **Divide powers between national and regional governments**
	- This is done in "federal countries," where it is divided into federations based on geography and/or cultural regions
	- Unitary countries (Britain/France) have one government for the whole country
	- Federal government governs matters that should be common across the whole state
		- Ex. national defence
	- Provincial/State government provide regulation for regions desiring distinct policies
		- Ex. Education
	- Local governments are even more granular (Ex. construction of roads)
	- Constitutions also set a process for how these branches can dispute over jurisdiction
3. **Delineate the limits of governmental power**
	- A constitution's existence, in many ways, is itself a limitation
	- Establishment of a list of base rights/liberties is common
		- Ex. The Bill of Rights, Charter of Rights and Freedoms
	- One common inclusion to this is freedom of religion
		- No level of government ha the power to take measures which limit its citizens right to religious freedom
4. **Orderly methods of constitutional change**
	- Political regimes aren't static, so an amendment process is necessary
## 2.2 Constitutional Forms: Conventions and Laws
- **Constitutional Conventions**: Rules which are enforced politically
	- Fear of political consequences designates the boundary
	- Sometimes these rules can be deferred, such as the delay of elections in 1940 Britain, when Churchill had high support.
- **Constitutional Law**: Rules which are enforced legally
	1. Organic Statutes
		- Establish constitutional rules, the organs of the regime
	2. Entrenched constitutional acts
		- Statutes dealing with the everyday matters - health care, traffic, etc.
		- These are like super-laws
		- The requirements for changing them are much higher
- **Social Contract**
	- John Locke
	- States that consent must be given to the government to rule, and then the government can disagree with the minority
- Entrenched acts are advantageous in some ways
	- Beyond the unilateral reach of state or federal governments
	- Can be more effective at guaranteeing citizens rights and liberties
	- No matter how much public support they have, politicians cannot legally adopt measures which violate entrenched guarantees
- Organic statues typically deal with a specific institution of the regime, while entrenched constitutional acts are generally more comprehensive
## 2.3 The Canadian Constitution
- Canada’s constitution is primarily a mixture of the British and American regimes
- America - embody many of our constitutional rules in entrenched acts
- British - reliant on constitutional conventions and organic statutes
- Canada’s two constitutional acts:
- **CA 1867**
	- Executive power
	- Legislative power
	- Federal division of power
	- Judicial power
- **CA 1982**
	- Charter of Rights and Freedoms
	- Indigenous Rights
	- Equalization and regional disparities
	- Amending formulas
	- Definition of the Canadian constitution
- CA 1867
- United 3 British colonies into the new “Dominion of Canada”
- The word dominion was invented to describe a regime that is neither a colony nor independent of the mother regime
- Canadians were still subjects of Britain, and under its judicial thumb
- Britain maintains control of Canada’s constitution among other decisions
- This is interesting, as it does not emanate from the will of the people, as most entrenched acts (which would come from a referendum)
- The constitution was legislated for them - it didn’t come from a Lockean ideal
- This act consists of a preamble and eleven parts
- Preamble: Notice of the conventions within it/
- Part 1: Official name of the act
    
- Part 2: Establish basic points on the union of the provinces
    
- Part 3/4: Outline executive and legislative powers for the Fed
    
- Part 5: Features of provincial constitutions (these have similar principles to the Fed)
    
- Part 6: Division of powers between the Provincial and Federal governments.
    
- Part 7: Rules to govern judicial power in Canada
    
- Part 8: Provisions on the Confederation’s financial details
    
- Part 9:  Miscellaneous provisions (e.g. French and English use)
    
- Part 10: Used as a guarantee that the Federal government would work on the Intercolonial Railway (Quebec to Halifax)
    
- Part 11: Procedures for admitting additional provinces
    

- CA 1982
    

- The British government offered patriation of the constitution into Canadian hands as early as the 1920s
    

- This was not taken seriously until post WWII (1940s), since Canadian politicians struggled to agree on a formula for amendments, which the British had handled up to that point.
    

- Many of the provinces leveraged patriation to make their own demands
    

- Ex. Quebec would only agree to patriation if some legislative jurisdictions would move from Ottawa to Quebec City
    

- Pierre Trudeau (PM) sought to link patriation to his Bill of Rights.
    

- With control of the Federal government, he tried to unilaterally push through the bill, although opposed by the “Gang of Eight” provinces
    

- This led to a Supreme Court case, which said that the Federal government could do it according to the law, but that it would diminish Provincial power and go against convention. Therefore, they had to get more provinces to agree
    

- Provinces would negotiate, since they now knew the Fed had ultimate power
    
- Fed would negotiate, to avoid the stigma from violating constitutional convention
    

- A compromise was reached (all prov. but Quebec agreed), which was signed by the British Parliament
    

- Canada Act, 1982, which created a second major constitutional law for Canada, the Constitution Act , 1982, comprised of 7 parts
    

- Part 1: Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms
    
- 2: Rights of Indigenous Peoples
    
- 3: Entrenches the principle of the Fed making equalization payments to provinces with revenues below the national average
    
- 4: Commit the governments/provinces/territories to hear out Indigenous concerns
    
- 5: Procedure for amending the constitution
    
- 6: Amendment to CA 1867, to strengthen powers of provincial governments in key areas (Ex. energy, forestry, etc.), a demand of the gang of 8
    
- 7: Miscellaneous provisions
    

## 2.4 Rights of Indigenous Peoples in Canada

- Two Categories of rights:
    
- Treaty Rights
    

- Legacy agreements between Canada and a specific group of Indigenous peoples
    
- This is not used now, as treaties are between two sovereign entities - but Canada recognizes itself as the only sovereign in Canada
    

- Existing Rights
    

- Rather than being created by a treaty itself, these rights are said to be inherent
    
- Especially rights regarding land and self-governance are important
    
- This creates a clear legal requirement for the negotiation of land claims agreements
    

- The Royal Proclamation of 1763 is a constitutional ancestor of the rights in CA 1982
    

- Formally assert British sovereign authority over lands occupied by indigenous people, and allows them to be ceded to the crown
    
- It also makes commitments to protect the Indigenous peoples
    
- From this, indigenous lands cannot be sold to private interests
    
- Indigenous peoples strongly rely on this document to protect them, yet simultaneously give their land rights to the crown

## 2.5 Amending Canada’s Constitution
- Part 5 of CA 1982 formulates constitutional amendments
	- Amendments made by:
		- Provincial Legislature --> Provincial constitutions
		- Parliament --> Executive/Legislative offices
		- Provincially entrenched/multi-province --> Concerned provinces and Parliament
		- Parliament + All provincial legislatures --> Matters of the Crown/French and English/Number of provincial MPs/Amending procedures
		- Federal Parliament + 2/3 of provincial legislatures --> General changes
## 2.6 Judicial Review of the Constitution
- Since there are so many possible situations, judicial review is key to informing outlier cases
- For example, *protection of the environment* may be federal, while *management of natural resources* is provincial. Who would resolve disputed over overlapping claims?
- Courts decide whose jurisdiction a legislative matter falls within
